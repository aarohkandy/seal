# SEAL Website Outline

This is a planning outline only. Do not build the full website until the larger implementation prompt arrives.

## Primary Goal

Create a credible, easy-to-understand public website for the Sensors, Energy, and Automation Laboratory that makes the lab look serious, active, and student-friendly while keeping the internal spreadsheet/game/dashboard machinery out of the public experience.

## Audience

- Prospective student researchers who want to join a project.
- Current members who need the right internal entry point.
- Faculty, sponsors, and industry partners who need to understand what SEAL does.
- Alumni and collaborators who want outcomes, publications, and contact paths.

## Core Navigation

1. Home
2. Research
3. Projects
4. Join SEAL
5. People
6. Publications and Outcomes
7. Internal Portal
8. Contact

## Page Outline

### 1. Home

Purpose: make SEAL legible in the first 10 seconds.

Content blocks:
- Hero: "Sensors, Energy, and Automation Laboratory" with a concise value line about applied research in sensing, energy, automation, and student-built systems.
- Primary calls to action: "Explore Projects" and "Join SEAL".
- Research area preview: sensing and embedded systems, energy and sustainability, automation and software, applied AI/data, aerospace/plasma, biomedical/environmental systems.
- Proof points: project count, active teams, publications, patents, internships, grants, or competitions only after the numbers are verified.
- Featured projects: 3 to 6 strong projects with real descriptions and images.
- Student pathway preview: apply, onboard, join a team, build portfolio-quality work.

Avoid:
- Internal jargon like Get Green, HP, Clan Life, KSS, battlestation, or quest mechanics.
- Grand claims that are not verified.
- Public links to raw sheets, dashboards, resumes, or Discord invites.

### 2. Research

Purpose: explain the lab's research scope with clean categories.

Suggested categories:
- Energy and sustainability: industrial assessment, energy efficiency, ULT freezer optimization, industrial symbiosis.
- Embedded sensing and instrumentation: PCB design, embedded systems, signal processing, sensor networks.
- Automation and applied software: dashboards, data pipelines, task systems, internal tooling, AI-supported workflows.
- Plasma, aerospace, and propulsion: DBD systems, aviation design, propulsion, UAV/QuietFlight work.
- Health, safety, and environment: PPG, wounds, water, air quality, radiation safety, medical devices.
- Research commercialization and writing: grants, business plans, patents, research papers, external networking.

Each research area should eventually include:
- Plain-English summary.
- What students work on.
- Example projects.
- Relevant skills.
- Contact or join CTA.

- One-sentence summary.
- Skills needed.
- Current status: recruiting, active, publishing, paused, archived.
- Outputs: paper, prototype, dataset, tool, competition, grant, patent.
- Contact/team owner, only if approved.

### 4. Join SEAL

Purpose: make the applicant path simple, respectful, and less spreadsheet-shaped.

Content blocks:
- Who should join: students interested in hands-on research, engineering, software, writing, design, entrepreneurship, sustainability, or commercialization.
- What students can do: build circuits, design embedded systems, run experiments, write papers, build software, analyze data, prototype devices, develop proposals, work with partners.
- Tracks:
  - Hardware and PCB.
  - Embedded systems and signal processing.
  - Energy and sustainability.
  - Software, AI, and data visualization.
  - Biomedical, environmental, and safety systems.
  - Technical writing, grants, patents, and business development.
  - Design, media, outreach, and education.
- Application steps:
  1. Review research areas.
  2. Submit interest form, resume, and short statement.
  3. Complete onboarding.
  4. Join a project/team.
  5. Build a portfolio and contribute to outputs.
- Expectations: weekly availability, communication norms, project follow-through, ethical data handling.
- CTA: "Apply to SEAL" or "Request project match".

Avoid:
- Public scoring, "lives", "red/yellow/green", HP, warnings, and rank systems.
- Overpromising outcomes like guaranteed jobs, visas, or elite admissions unless formally approved.

### 5. People

Purpose: show legitimacy and make roles understandable.

Content blocks:
- Faculty director.
- Staff/advisors.
- Current team leads, only with consent.
- Alumni/affiliate highlights, only with consent.
- Optional filtered directory by research area.

Rules:
- Never pull names, emails, photos, GPAs, resumes, transcripts, or phone numbers directly from the PDFs.
- Publish only approved profiles.

### 6. Publications and Outcomes

Purpose: make accomplishments visible without turning the site into a reward spreadsheet.

Content blocks:
- Publications.
- Patents or invention disclosures.
- Grants and funded projects.
- Competitions and awards.
- Student outcomes: internships, jobs, graduate school, conference travel, portfolio work.

Tone:
- Specific and verifiable.
- No vague hype.
- No internal rank/reward ladder unless reframed as general support.

### 7. Internal Portal

Purpose: give current members one clean launchpad without exposing private systems.

Suggested modules:
- Dashboard.
- Scheduler.
- Project/quest board.
- Training program.
- Documentation/how-to.
- Reference letter and recognition requests.
- Team resources.

Access:
- Public page can describe that an internal portal exists.
- Actual links should be protected, authenticated, or distributed through approved channels.

### 8. Contact

Purpose: make next actions obvious.

Content blocks:
- General inquiry.
- Student application.
- Partner/sponsor inquiry.
- Press/media inquiry.
- Location/lab affiliation.

## Content Triage

Public:
- Mission, research areas, approved project summaries, approved people, verified outcomes, application pathway.

Internal but useful:
- Scheduler, dashboards, quest/project operations, training, task status, rank/reward process, documentation links.

Discard or heavily rewrite:
- Spreadsheet instructions as public content.
- Gamified status mechanics.
- Diagnostics/warnings.
- Private applicant/member rows.
- Any resume, transcript, GPA, email, phone, personal goals, or internal review language.

## Design Direction Placeholder

Wait for the larger prompt before choosing a stack or visual system. The likely direction should be serious, technical, and easy to scan: fewer gimmicks, more project evidence, strong typography, clear cards/tables, and a polished join flow.
